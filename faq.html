Colon Cancer — Q & A
Common questions from patients and families, answered by Dr. Rigved Nittala
Doctor, what exactly is colon cancer? Is it the same as stomach cancer?
No, these are different cancers. Colon cancer (or colorectal cancer) affects the large intestine (colon) or the rectum — the last part of your digestive system. Stomach cancer affects the stomach, which is higher up. They have different causes, symptoms, and treatments. The colon is roughly 1.5 metres long and its job is to form and expel stool. When cells in the colon wall grow abnormally and out of control, that is colon cancer.
I sometimes see blood in my stool. I assumed it's piles. Should I be worried?
This is perhaps the most important question. Blood in the stool should NEVER be automatically assumed to be from haemorrhoids (piles) without a proper medical evaluation.
While piles are far more common, colon cancer can also cause rectal bleeding. The only way to know for sure is to see a doctor. A simple stool test (FIT test) or a physical examination can start the investigation. Do not self-diagnose or self-medicate. If you have had blood in your stool more than once, please see a doctor this week.
Does colon cancer only happen to old people? I am 35 — should I be concerned?
In Western countries, colon cancer mostly affects people over 65. But in India, the story is different. Studies from major cancer centres show that Indian patients tend to develop colon cancer significantly younger — the average age at diagnosis in India is around 47, which is more than a decade earlier than in the West. Young-onset colon cancer is a recognised and increasing problem in India.

So while being 35 does not mean you need routine screening, it does mean you should not ignore symptoms. If you have any of the warning signs — blood in stool, persistent bowel changes, unexplained weight loss — please see a doctor regardless of your age.
My father had colon cancer. Am I at higher risk?
Yes, significantly so. Having a first-degree relative (parent, sibling, or child) with colon cancer roughly doubles your risk. If two first-degree relatives are affected, or if your relative was diagnosed under age 50, your risk is even higher.

Genetic conditions like Lynch Syndrome (also called HNPCC) and Familial Adenomatous Polyposis (FAP) can run in families and greatly elevate risk. If you have a family history, you should discuss this with your doctor and consider starting screening earlier — potentially from age 40 or even earlier depending on the family pattern. NICE guidelines now require all new colon cancer diagnoses to be tested for Lynch Syndrome.
What is a colonoscopy? Is it painful? I am scared.
A colonoscopy is a procedure where a doctor inserts a thin, flexible tube with a camera (a colonoscope) through your back passage to examine the inside of your colon. The thought of it sounds uncomfortable, but in practice, it is performed under sedation — you will be relaxed, drowsy, or asleep, and will feel very little.

Most people say the preparation (drinking a laxative solution the day before to clear the bowel) is the most inconvenient part. The procedure itself takes about 20–45 minutes. If a polyp is found, it is often removed during the same procedure, which could prevent cancer from ever developing. It truly is a life-saving investigation and far less frightening than it sounds.
Is there a simpler test before colonoscopy? I am not ready for a camera.
Yes. Not popular in India, but the Faecal Immunochemical Test (FIT) is a simple, at-home stool test that checks for tiny amounts of blood in your stool that you cannot see with the naked eye. You collect a small sample at home and send it to a lab. It is painless, non-invasive, and costs very little.

NICE guidelines recommend it as the first step for anyone with bowel symptoms. If the FIT test is positive (blood detected), then a colonoscopy is arranged. If negative, your doctor will advise further steps based on your symptoms.
If I eat lots of vegetables and am not overweight, can I still get colon cancer?
Yes, healthy lifestyle choices significantly reduce your risk but do not eliminate it entirely. Colon cancer can develop due to genetic factors, family history, or random cellular mutations that have nothing to do with lifestyle. This is why even healthy individuals should undergo screening from age 45, or earlier if they have a family history or symptoms.

Diet and lifestyle are powerful prevention tools, but they are not a guarantee. Regular screening is the safety net.
If I am diagnosed with colon cancer, will I need a permanent colostomy bag?
This is one of the most common fears patients express to me, and the reassuring answer is: not usually. With modern surgical techniques including laparoscopic (keyhole) surgery, most colon cancer patients do not need a permanent stoma (colostomy bag). A temporary bag may be used while the bowel heals after surgery, but this is often reversed.

The need for a permanent colostomy is more common with very low rectal cancers, and even then, improved surgical techniques have significantly reduced this requirement. Your surgical team will discuss this in detail before any operation.
Can colon cancer be completely cured?
Absolutely — when caught early. Stage I colon cancer, which has not spread beyond the colon wall, has a five-year survival rate greater than 90%. Even Stage II and III have excellent outcomes with appropriate treatment combining surgery and chemotherapy.
Stage IV (metastatic) cancer is harder to cure but treatments have improved significantly. Immunotherapy, targeted therapy, and selected surgical removal of liver metastases have extended survival considerably. The key message: early detection gives you the best chance at a complete cure.
I live in a small town. Can I access colon cancer care there?
Colon cancer awareness and early detection is improving across India, but access to advanced care remains uneven. If a colonoscopy is needed, district or government hospitals in most state capitals have endoscopy facilities. For surgery and chemotherapy, referral to a cancer centre may be needed.

The Ayushman Bharat PM-JAY scheme & Private Health Insurance cover many cancer treatments, including surgery and chemotherapy. Do not assume treatment is out of reach — seek a referral from your primary doctor.

"Doctor, Is This Serious?" — Mouth Cancer, Answered
Questions about oral cavity cancer from patients and families
My mother has a white patch inside her mouth. She's had it for 2 months. Should we be worried?
Yes, this needs to be evaluated. A white patch that doesn't wipe off and lasts more than 3 weeks is called leukoplakia. It is a pre-cancerous condition. Most leukoplakias do not become cancer, but some do — and a doctor cannot tell which ones just by looking.
Your mother needs a visit to a surgical oncologist or an oral medicine specialist for examination and possibly a biopsy. Please don't delay this.
My husband has been chewing gutka for 20 years. He seems fine. Does he really need to stop?
I understand why it feels that way — habits that don't cause immediate pain often feel harmless. But by the time you feel pain or see something obviously wrong in oral cancer, the disease has often already progressed.

Twenty years of gutka use places your husband at significantly elevated risk. The carcinogens in gutka and areca nut are Class 1 carcinogens — the same category as asbestos. Stopping now will not erase all past damage, but it will meaningfully reduce the risk going forward.

I would also recommend he see a doctor for a mouth examination.
My father was diagnosed with Stage 3 tongue cancer. The doctor says he needs jaw surgery and reconstruction. Is this really necessary?
I know this sounds frightening, and the word 'reconstruction' can feel overwhelming. But here is the reality: for Stage 3 tongue cancer, surgery is the most effective treatment. Modern reconstruction — using tissue from the forearm or thigh to rebuild the tongue and floor of the mouth — gives excellent functional results.

Most patients are able to speak and eat near-normally within months.

The alternative — not operating — gives the cancer more time to spread, and Stage 4 disease is much harder to treat. Get a second opinion at a major cancer centre if you wish, but please do not delay the decision.
Can we treat mouth cancer only with radiation? My father doesn't want surgery.
For oral cavity cancers, surgery is the preferred first treatment according to Indian clinical practice guidelines and international standards.

Radiation alone is an option for patients who truly cannot undergo surgery for medical reasons, but outcomes are generally better with surgery. Radiation as the sole treatment for large oral tumours can cause significant side effects including dry mouth, difficulty swallowing, and jaw problems.

The decision must be made by a multidisciplinary team. If your father is reluctant, I would strongly encourage a detailed discussion with his surgical oncologist about what surgery actually involves — many fears are based on outdated information.
Is oral cancer covered under Ayushman Bharat (PM-JAY)?
Yes. Oral cancer surgery, radiation therapy, and chemotherapy are covered under the Pradhan Mantri Jan Arogya Yojana (PM-JAY) for eligible families.
What are the chances of recovery if the cancer is detected early?
Very good. Stage I oral cavity cancer has a 5-year survival rate of over 80%. Stage II is also associated with good outcomes with appropriate surgery. The key word is 'early'.
In India, the tragedy is that over 80% of patients present at Stage 3 or 4, when survival rates drop to 30–50%. This is not because the cancer is more aggressive in India — it is because of delayed diagnosis.

If you catch it early, the surgery is smaller, the recovery is faster, and the chances of cure are high.
My uncle had his tongue partially removed. Will he ever speak or eat normally again?
With modern rehabilitation, many patients who have had partial tongue removal (hemi-glossectomy) regain functional speech and swallowing.

It requires dedicated speech therapy and swallowing rehabilitation, which are routinely offered at good cancer centres. The extent of recovery depends on how much of the tongue was removed and whether reconstruction was done.

Patients I have operated on have returned to their jobs, social lives, and in many cases, to eating normally. It takes time and effort — usually 3 to 6 months — but life does go on.
My cousin is 32 years old and has a sore on her tongue that hasn't healed for 6 weeks. She doesn't smoke or chew tobacco. Can she still have cancer?
Yes, though it is less common. While tobacco and areca nut account for the majority of oral cancers in India, cancer can occur in non-users.

HPV infection, chronic trauma (from a sharp tooth or ill-fitting denture), and genetic factors can all contribute.

A non-healing ulcer for 6 weeks in any person — regardless of habits — must be biopsied. Please take her to a surgical oncologist as soon as possible. Six weeks is already longer than the recommended 3-week trigger for evaluation.
What can I do to protect my family from mouth cancer?
First, if anyone in your family chews gutka, pan masala, khaini, or uses tobacco in any form — encourage them to stop today. Not reduce, stop.

Second, check your own mouth in a mirror once a month. Look at the tongue (top and underside), inner cheeks, and gums.

Third, visit a dentist at least once a year — they can spot early lesions.

Fourth, know the warning signs and don't wait more than 3 weeks before seeking evaluation.

Fifth, talk about it. Oral cancer is deeply stigmatised because it's associated with habits people feel ashamed of. That stigma costs lives. The more openly we talk, the sooner people seek help.
