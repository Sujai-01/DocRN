Colon Cancer in India: What Every Family Needs to Know

Dr. Rigved Nittala  ·  24 March 2026  ·  10 min read  ·  Evidence-based · India-specific · Actionable

In my practice, one of the questions I hear most often is: "Could my stomach problems be something serious?" In most cases, the answer is reassuring. But every year, I see patients who waited too long before asking that question. Colon cancer — cancer of the large intestine — is a disease where time truly matters. Detected early, it is almost always curable. Detected late, it is often not.

India recorded over 64,000 new colorectal cancer cases in 2022, resulting in nearly 38,000 deaths. As a nation, we are seeing a sharp, sustained rise in these numbers. As your doctor, I want to break this down in a way that is practical and relevant for you and your family.

Understanding the Anatomy: What Is the Colon?

The colon (or large intestine) is approximately 1.5 metres long and sits in your abdomen. Its job is to absorb water and electrolytes from undigested food, and to form and expel stool. Colon cancer typically begins in the inner lining of the colon wall as an abnormal polyp — a small clump of cells — which, if not detected and removed, can transform into a full cancer over 10 to 15 years.

This long window is exactly why screening works so well. If we find and remove polyps early, we can prevent cancer from ever forming.

India-Specific Data You Should Know

  • 64,863 new cases and 38,367 deaths in India in 2022 (GLOBOCAN 2022)
  • Colon cancer is now the 4th most common cancer in India for both sexes combined
  • CRC incidence in India rose by 20% from 2004 to 2014
  • Urban areas show rates nearly double those of rural regions
  • Mean patient age at Tata Memorial Hospital: 47.2 years — more than a decade younger than typical Western patients
  • The majority of Indian patients present with locally advanced or metastatic disease, leading to significantly lower survival rates

Risk Factors: Who Is at Greater Risk?

  • Age 45+ (average risk)
  • Family history: A parent, sibling, or child with colorectal cancer roughly doubles your risk. Genetic syndromes (FAP, Lynch Syndrome) elevate it further
  • Diet: High red meat, processed foods, low fibre, high sugar
  • Medical conditions: IBD (Crohn's disease, Ulcerative Colitis), Type 2 Diabetes, obesity

Recognising the Symptoms

Based on NICE 2023 updated guidelines and Indian clinical experience, seek urgent medical evaluation if you have any of the following:

  • Blood in your stools — This should never be assumed to be from haemorrhoids (piles) without evaluation
  • Change in bowel habits lasting more than 4 weeks — Diarrhoea, constipation, or thinner stools
  • Unexplained weight loss
  • Persistent lower abdominal pain, cramps, or bloating
  • Feeling that your bowel has not emptied fully
  • Unexplained fatigue or breathlessness — Often caused by anaemia from slow bleeding
  • Iron-deficiency anaemia without an obvious cause
NICE 2023 guidelines specifically recommend a Faecal Immunochemical Test (FIT) as the first investigation for all adults with these symptoms — a simple, painless, at-home stool test. A positive FIT result requires urgent colonoscopy referral within 2 weeks. Note: There is still no Indian data on FIT as a uniform population-based screening test.

Diagnosis: What to Expect

  • Colonoscopy: The gold standard. Polyps, if found, can be removed at the same time. Tissue biopsies are taken from any suspicious areas
  • CT Colonography: A radiation-based scan used when colonoscopy is not possible
  • Blood tests: Full blood count (for anaemia), CEA (tumour marker), liver function tests
  • Staging CT/MRI: To determine if cancer has spread to lymph nodes, liver, or lungs

Treatment: Modern Options Available in India

  • Stage I-II (Early): Surgery alone is usually curative. Five-year survival exceeds 80-90%
  • Stage III (Locally advanced): Surgery combined with adjuvant chemotherapy (FOLFOX or CAPOX regimens). Five-year survival around 60-70%
  • Stage IV (Metastatic): Chemotherapy, targeted therapy (bevacizumab, cetuximab based on RAS/BRAF testing), and immunotherapy (pembrolizumab, nivolumab for MSI-High tumours). Selected patients with liver-only metastases may benefit from surgical resection

Prevention: Your Daily Choices Matter

  • Eat more fibre: Whole grains, brown rice, oats, sabzi, dal every day
  • Prioritise vegetables and fruits over packaged snacks
  • Limit red and processed meats: Reduce mutton, beef, sausages, and salami intake
  • Cut sugar and maida — associated with obesity, which is a CRC risk factor
  • Walk 30-45 minutes a day: Physical inactivity is a modifiable risk factor
  • Avoid tobacco in all forms; moderate alcohol use
  • Keep your weight in check: BMI above 30 significantly increases risk

A Personal Note

As a doctor, I see too many patients who come to me after months of ignoring symptoms. The most common reason? They assumed the blood in the stool was from piles, or that the bloating was just gas. I understand — these conversations are uncomfortable. But I urge you: do not let embarrassment delay a lifesaving investigation.

Colon cancer is not a death sentence when found early. It is a highly manageable, often completely curable disease. Knowledge is your first line of defence.
